# The TodayIsTheDay Method — Fighting Procrastination

> English version. Wersja polska: [`titd-method.pl.md`](./titd-method.pl.md)

This document describes the anti-procrastination method used in the **TodayIsTheDay (TITD)** app,
compares it with evidence-based techniques, and recommends three simple improvements.
Guiding principle: **keep the method simple and easy to use — don't over-engineer it.**

---

## Part 1 — The TITD Method

TITD is an app built on **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)**, using a "micro-self-management"
approach — short, simple daily challenges/lessons.

### Method principles

1. **Hyper-personalization at start** — an initial quiz identifies habits, triggers, and
   procrastination patterns; a personalized plan and goals are built from it.
2. **One short lesson/challenge per day** — a few-minute task, easy to fit into a routine;
   deliberately only one per day to avoid overload and force reflection.
3. **Progressive difficulty** — challenges go from easiest to hardest, adapting to progress,
   without feeling overwhelmed.
4. **Breaking tasks into small steps** — intimidating tasks split into manageable stages.
5. **The two-minute rule** — if a task takes less than 2 minutes, do it immediately;
   prevents accumulation of small tasks.
6. **Realistic SMART goals** — Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound.
7. **Prioritization** — a daily planner orders tasks and helps finish them on time.
8. **Positive reinforcement and affirmations** — acknowledging progress, self-compassion over
   pressure, mentally rewarding yourself for each small win.
9. **Journaling and daily reflection** — logging emotions, challenges, and successes; reflective
   questions at the end of each lesson to understand procrastination triggers.
10. **Progress tracking** — visualizing what's done and what remains.
11. **Community / accountability support** — a forum of people with similar struggles.

### Philosophy

- No promise of overnight success — the emphasis is on **consistency and gradual progress**.
- Changing habits without sacrificing well-being.
- The user is the agent of change; the app is a tool guiding them step by step.

> **Methodological note:** the material comes from the official site and partly
> marketing/affiliate reviews. Treat the claimed "95% success rate" as promotional material,
> not verified data.

## Part 2 — Comparative research (evidence-based)

Key research finding (Sirois & Pychyl 2013; Steel 2007; Pychyl): **procrastination is an emotion
regulation problem, not a time management problem** — "short-term mood repair at the expense of
long-term goals". TITD's philosophy (CBT + micro-self-management) is consistent with this.

| Technique | Scientific source | Does TITD already have it? |
|---|---|---|
| Two-minute rule / micro-goals | Fogg 2009; Amabile (progress principle) 2011 | ✅ Yes |
| Personalization / trigger identification | Steel 2007; Ferrari 2010 | ✅ Yes (quiz) |
| SMART goals, prioritization | — | ✅ Yes |
| Positive reinforcement | — | ✅ Yes (affirmations) |
| Self-compassion after a lapse | Wohl, Pychyl & Bennett 2010 | ⚠️ Partially |
| Implementation intentions (IF-THEN) | Gollwitzer & Brandstätter 1997 | ❌ Missing |
| Naming the emotion before a task | Sirois & Pychyl 2013 | ⚠️ Partially (journaling) |
| Temptation bundling | Milkman 2014 | ❌ Missing |
| Environmental design | Thaler & Sunstein 2008 | ❌ Missing |
| Structured procrastination | Perry 2011 | ❌ Missing |

## Part 3 — Recommended improvements (exactly 3)

Each improvement fits the "one short challenge per day" formula, has strong research support,
and **does not increase the method's complexity.**

### 1. Implementation intention (IF-THEN)

- **What it is:** a single sentence — *"When [specific situation], I will [specific action]."*
  E.g. "When I sit down at my desk after morning coffee, I will open the report and write the first sentence."
- **Why it works:** Gollwitzer & Brandstätter (1997) — pre-deciding *when* and *where* removes the
  decision point the brain uses as an excuse to delay; significantly raises follow-through.
- **How to fit into TITD:** add one field to the daily challenge — the user writes one IF-THEN
  sentence. Zero extra steps.

### 2. Quick emotion check before starting

- **What it is:** 1 question before the task — *"What am I feeling about this task?"*
  (fear / boredom / overwhelm / resentment / ambiguity).
- **Why it works:** Sirois & Pychyl (2013) — procrastination is avoidance of an uncomfortable
  emotion. Naming the emotion targets the real cause, not the symptom ("no time").
- **How to fit into TITD:** extend the existing journaling with one question asked *before*
  starting, not after.

### 3. Self-compassion after a lapse

- **What it is:** an explicit rule — *"Skipped it? Forgive yourself without judgment and come back tomorrow."*
- **Why it works:** Wohl, Pychyl & Bennett (2010) — self-forgiveness after procrastinating *reduces*
  future procrastination, while self-criticism increases it (guilt spiral → avoidance → more guilt).
- **How to fit into TITD:** TITD has affirmations but no explicit rule for the moment of failure —
  which is the moment of highest spiral risk. Add a short, non-judgmental message after a missed day.

## Part 4 — Deliberately rejected (keeping it simple)

The techniques below are effective but add complexity without proportional benefit for a simple,
daily method. **Rejected on purpose, to avoid over-engineering:**

- **Temptation bundling** (Milkman 2014) — requires pairing rewards, complicates the daily flow.
- **Environmental design** (Thaler & Sunstein 2008) — outside the scope of a "one challenge per day" app.
- **Structured procrastination** (Perry 2011) — conflicts with simplicity; requires task-list management.
- **The 5-procrastinator-types typology** (Ferrari; Steel) — diagnostically valuable but too heavy
  for a lightweight method; partly covered by the emotion check (improvement #2).
